Module-LWE with Hints Zoo

Inspired by Martin Albrecht's SIS with Hints Zoo, this website summarizes variants of the Module-LWE problem allowing for additional hints on the error and/or secret.
While maybe less exotic, the Module-LWE zoo seems to be less prone to attacks ;-)
When listing relevant references, I tried to keep a chronical order to the best of my knowledge.
If you have any comments or know about a reference I missed, please write me an e-mail.
Scope
Only results looking at the structured Module-LWE variants are listed.
All of them can be instantiated with rings of degree \(d=1\), leading to the unstructured LWE case.
Hence, results that are (so far) only proven for the plain LWE case, and not for the more general Module-LWE, are not (yet) discussed.
Some of them can be instantiated with rank \(n=1\), leading to the Ring-LWE case.
Mathematical Setup
Let \(\ring\) be the ring of integers of a number field of degree \(d\).
Ler \(q\) be a positive integers, called the modulus.
Let \(m\) and \(n\) be positive integers, respectively defining the number of rows and columns of the matrix \(\mat{A} \in \ring^{m \times n}\).
By \(\mat{I}_n\), we denote the identity matrix of dimension \( n \times n \).
When looking at distributions like discrete Gaussians over \(\ring\), we need to specify the embedding (to the real numbers) we are using.
For simplicity, I omit this specification, which is can be found in the respective reference.

Approximate Linear Hints

Given \(\mat{A} \sample \ring_{q}^{m \times n}\), \(\vec{b}= [\mat{A} | \mat{I}_n] \vec{x} \bmod q\ring\), \(\mat{M}\) and \(\vec{z}=\mat{M}\vec{x} + \vec{f}\),
where \(\vec{x}\) follows some distribution \(D_{\vec{x}}\) and \(\vec{f}\) follows some distribution \(D_{\vec{f}}\).
Depending on the concrete formulation, the matrix \(\mat{M}\) might either be sampled from some distribution or be adversarially chosen, potentially only after having seen \(\mat{A}\).

The search version asks to find \(\vec{x}\).
The decision version asks to distinguish \(\mat{A},\vec{b},\mat{M},\vec{z}\) from a sample where \(\vec{b}\) is sampled uniformly at random.

Hardness
So far, all reductions assume both \(\vec{x}\) and \(\vec{f}\) to follow a discrete Gaussian distribution.
The reason for this is the use of Gaussian convolution properties to describe the distribution of \(\vec{x}\) conditioned on the leakage \(\mat{M},\mat{M}\vec{x}+\vec{f}\).
The most general formulation is provided in [ENP24]. Their reduction shows that Module-LWE with approximate linear hints is at least as hard as standard Module-LWE (i.e., where no hints are given out).
While preserving the ring degree \(d\), the matrix dimensions \(m\) and \(n\), as well as the modulus \(q\), the incurred reduction loss is reflected by requiring a larger Gaussian width of the starting standard Module-LWE problem.
The required gap depends on the Gaussian widths of \(\vec{x}\) and \(\vec{f}\) of the resulting Module-LWE with approximate linear hints problem, as well as on the norm \( \Vert \mat{M} \Vert_2 \) of the hint matrix \(\mat{M}\).
They cover both the search and the decision versions.
